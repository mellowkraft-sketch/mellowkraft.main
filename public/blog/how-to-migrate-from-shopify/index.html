How to Migrate from Shopify Without Losing Orders, SEO, or Your Mind

By MellowKraft  ·  March 2025  ·  9 min read

The hardest part of moving off Shopify isn't the technical work. It's the fear of something going wrong during the switch — orders getting lost, Google rankings dropping, customer data disappearing.

None of these need to happen. Migrations fail when they're rushed or poorly planned. With the right preparation, a migration from Shopify to a custom platform can be invisible to customers and safe for your SEO.

This is the complete checklist we use at MellowKraft. Whether you're migrating yourself or working with an agency, this is what a responsible migration looks like.

Before You Start: What to Export from Shopify

Shopify gives you data access via their Admin API and CSV exports. Before you write a single line of new code, export everything:

  • ✓Products — all SKUs, variants, prices, images, metafields, inventory levels
  • ✓Customers — name, email, phone, address, order history, tags
  • ✓Orders — all historical orders, line items, fulfillment status, financial status
  • ✓Discounts & gift cards — active codes, redemption counts
  • ✓Pages & blogs — all CMS content with slugs preserved
  • ✓Redirects — any existing 301 redirects you've set up in Shopify
  • ✓URL map — every product, collection, and page URL that exists today

The URL map is critical. Shopify uses a specific URL structure: /products/product-handle, /collections/collection-handle. If your new platform uses different URL patterns, you need a redirect plan before go-live.

The SEO Risk (and How to Eliminate It)

The #1 fear about migrating off Shopify is losing Google rankings. This is a legitimate concern — but it's entirely preventable with proper redirects and no preventable if you rush.

The most common SEO mistake in platform migrations: going live without setting up 301 redirects from old Shopify URLs to new platform URLs. Google sees the old pages returning 404, de-indexes them, and your rankings drop. This is not a Shopify problem — it's a planning problem.

The redirect protocol

  1. Export the complete URL map from your Shopify store
  2. Map each old URL to its equivalent on the new platform
  3. Implement 301 redirects at the server/CDN level before DNS cutover
  4. Test every redirect before going live
  5. Submit updated sitemap to Google Search Console immediately after cutover
  6. Monitor Google Search Console for crawl errors for 4 weeks post-migration

If you maintain your URL structure (same handles, same patterns), the redirect work is minimal. If you change URL structures, plan for 3–6 months of traffic fluctuation as Google re-crawls and re-indexes.

What else protects your SEO

  • Transfer all page titles and meta descriptions to the new platform exactly
  • Port all structured data (JSON-LD schemas) to equivalent pages
  • Ensure the new platform's sitemap is submitted before Shopify is taken offline
  • Keep Shopify running in read-only mode for 2–4 weeks after cutover as a fallback

The Payment Cutover: The Most Critical Moment

This is where most migrating brands get anxious — and rightfully so. The moment when payment processing switches from one platform to another is the highest-risk window.

The safest approach:

  1. Set up and test your new payment gateway integration thoroughly in staging
  2. Run real test transactions with small amounts across all payment methods
  3. Schedule the cutover for your lowest-traffic window (typically 2–4am, mid-week)
  4. Have a rollback plan: if new payments fail, DNS can be rolled back to Shopify within minutes
  5. Monitor the first 50 successful orders manually before declaring success

In India specifically, test your Razorpay or PayU integration against: UPI, credit card, debit card, net banking, and COD. Each payment method has its own edge cases.

Data Migration: What's Harder Than It Looks

Customer passwords

You cannot migrate customer passwords from Shopify. Shopify stores passwords hashed, and does not expose them via API (correctly, for security reasons). Your options: trigger a "set new password" email to all customers after migration, or allow social login (Google, email OTP) as the primary auth method on the new platform.

Order history

Historical orders should be imported as read-only records — customers should be able to see their order history from the new platform. This requires mapping Shopify order IDs to your new system's IDs and preserving all line items, totals, and fulfillment status.

Product metafields

If you've used Shopify metafields for custom product data (fit guides, fabric details, care instructions), these need to be explicitly exported and mapped to your new platform's data model. Standard CSV exports don't include metafields — you need to use the Admin API.

The 6-Week Migration Timeline

  • Week 1

    Audit & Architecture

    Complete data export. Map all URLs. Document every Shopify app and its function. Design the new platform data model. Define redirect plan.

  • Week 2

    Core Platform Build

    Set up infrastructure. Build product catalog, cart, and checkout. Integrate payment gateways. Implement order management.

  • Week 3

    Data Migration & Storefront

    Import all products, customers, and historical orders. Build storefront. Implement all redirects. Port all SEO metadata.

  • Week 4

    Testing & Staging

    Full end-to-end testing on staging environment. Test all payment methods. Test all edge cases in checkout and fulfillment. Fix bugs.

  • Week 5

    Soft Launch

    Go live with new platform. Monitor closely. Keep Shopify in standby. Process first 200 orders and verify everything manually.

  • Week 6

    Decommission & Handover

    Cancel Shopify subscription. Submit updated sitemap to Google. Monitor GSC for crawl errors. Complete team training on new platform.

What to Tell Your Team and Customers

Internal: brief your ops, marketing, and customer support teams before go-live. They'll be using a new system — make sure they know what changed and what stayed the same. A 1-hour walkthrough before cutover prevents most post-launch confusion.

Customers: you don't need to announce a platform migration proactively. Most customers will never notice. The exception: if you're migrating customer accounts, send a brief email asking them to set a new password. Keep the message simple — "We've upgraded our platform for a better experience. Please set a new password here."

The clearest sign a migration went well: customers don't know it happened. The clearest sign it didn't: your ops team gets flooded with "my order is missing" support tickets in the first week.

Post-Migration: The First 30 Days

  • ✓Monitor Google Search Console daily for crawl errors and coverage issues
  • ✓Compare weekly order volume and conversion rate against pre-migration baseline
  • ✓Check that all payment gateway reconciliation is working correctly
  • ✓Verify all fulfillment integrations (Shiprocket, Delhivery, etc.) are firing correctly
  • ✓Monitor server error rates and latency — new infrastructure sometimes surfaces under real load
  • ✓Cancel Shopify subscription only after 30 days of stable operation
